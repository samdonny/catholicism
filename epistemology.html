Epistemology: Scripture, Tradition & Authority

Introductory Summary

Epistemology asks how we come to know divine truth and which authorities can bind our conscience. In the sixteenth century the Reformers argued that the Bible alone is the ultimate source of Christian doctrine (known as sola scriptura), whereas Catholicism teaches that divine revelation comes to us through both Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition, authoritatively interpreted by the living Magisterium of the Church. This foundational difference underlies many other doctrinal disputes.

What the Topic Is

Theological epistemology deals with the sources of revelation and the criterion for judging doctrinal truth. Protestants maintain that everything necessary for salvation is contained in the canonical Scriptures and that nothing is to be added by new revelation or tradition; the Westminster Confession teaches that the authority of Scripture “dependeth not upon the testimony of any man, or Church, but wholly upon God” and that the whole counsel of God concerning salvation “is either expressly set down in Scripture, or by good and necessary consequence may be deduced from Scripture; unto which nothing at any time is to be added, whether by new revelations of the Spirit, or traditions of men.”[1] Catholics, by contrast, hold that Christ entrusted both the written word and the oral apostolic tradition to the Church and that these form one inseparable deposit of faith; Vatican II teaches that Sacred Tradition and Sacred Scripture are “bound closely together, and communicate one with the other” and that the task of giving an authentic interpretation of the Word of God “has been entrusted to the living teaching office of the Church.”[3]

Why It Matters

How we answer the question of authority influences every other theological dispute. If Scripture alone is the supreme and sufficient rule of faith, then doctrines or practices not found explicitly in Scripture are at best of secondary importance. If, however, Scripture and Tradition together form a single deposit of revelation under the guardianship of the Magisterium, then extra‑biblical teachings like the canon of Scripture, the Trinity and infant baptism stand on the same authoritative footing as biblical passages. A Catholic and a Protestant can both appeal to the Bible, but they will read it within different frameworks of authority.

Doctrinal Summary

Protestant Position

The classical Reformed statement of sola scriptura appears in the Westminster Confession. It insists that the authority of Holy Scripture is derived solely from God and that the entire counsel of God necessary for salvation is contained in Scripture alone. It explicitly rejects any addition to Scripture by new revelations or human traditions and declares that “the supreme judge, by which all controversies of religion are to be determined…can be no other but the Holy Spirit speaking in the Scripture.”[2] For Protestants, creeds and councils have ministerial authority only insofar as they repeat biblical teaching.

Catholic Position

Catholicism teaches that God’s Word comes to us through both Scripture and Tradition, transmitted from the apostles to the Church. Vatican II’s constitution Dei Verbum states that these two sources “make up a single sacred deposit of the word of God” and cannot be separated; the Magisterium (the pope and bishops in communion with him) serves as the “authentic interpreter” of that deposit.[3] The Council of Trent warned against personal interpretation of Scripture contrary to the “sense held by Holy Mother Church” and required ecclesial approval for publishing translations or commentaries.[4]

The Protestant Argument

Protestants argue that Scripture alone is uniquely inspired and therefore uniquely infallible. Because all humans are fallible, any allegedly unwritten tradition is subject to error; consequently, doctrines must be proved from the Bible alone. They observe that Jesus rebuked the Pharisees for elevating human traditions above God’s commandments and that even Church councils have contradicted one another. The Westminster divines therefore made Scripture the sole norma normans (norming norm) and located the Church’s authority in its faithful proclamation of Scripture. On this view:

  • The Bible alone is God‑breathed and thus the final court of appeal;
  • The Church’s authority is ministerial and declarative, not magisterial;
  • Traditions may be useful but must be tested against Scripture;
  • The Holy Spirit speaks through Scripture to judge doctrinal controversies.[2]

Any doctrine that cannot be shown from Scripture (explicitly or by good and necessary consequence) is therefore considered non‑binding.

The Catholic Argument

Catholics contend that the Bible arose within the Church and that the same authority which identified the canon continues to safeguard its correct interpretation. The New Testament never instructs Christians to operate by Scripture alone; rather, St Paul commands believers to “stand firm and hold to the traditions” they were taught orally or by letter (2 Th 2:15). Early Christian writers consistently appealed to apostolic tradition to refute heresies. Tertullian formulated a rule of faith: because Christ sent the apostles to teach, no one should be received as a preacher unless he can trace his message to them; true doctrine is recognised by agreement with the apostolic churches, “the moulds and original sources of the faith,” and all doctrines contrary to that apostolic tradition must be rejected.[5] Irenaeus likewise argued that one can trace the succession of bishops back to the apostles and that the Church at Rome holds a pre‑eminent authority with which all churches must agree.[6]

Vatican II therefore teaches that Scripture, Tradition and the Magisterium are “so linked and joined together that one cannot stand without the others.”[3] Without an authoritative interpreter, Catholics argue, the text of Scripture is susceptible to private interpretations, leading to the doctrinal fragmentation seen in Protestantism. The Church’s teaching office exists not to add to God’s revelation but to safeguard it and to transmit it faithfully through the centuries. This harmonises with the practice of the early Church and provides a stable basis for doctrinal development.
