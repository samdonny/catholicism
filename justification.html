Justification: Faith, Works & Salvation

Introductory Summary

The doctrine of justification addresses how sinners are made right with God. During the Reformation Martin Luther insisted that we are justified by faith alone (sola fide) and not by works, whereas the Catholic Church affirmed that faith must work through charity and that justification includes an interior renewal. Because justification concerns our eternal destiny, this issue lies at the heart of the divide between Catholics and Protestants.

What the Topic Is

In Protestant theology, justification is primarily forensic: God declares the sinner righteous on account of Christ’s righteousness imputed to him. Sanctification is a separate process of moral transformation that follows justification. The Westminster Confession states that faith “receiveth and resteth on Christ and his righteousness” and is the “alone instrument of justification,” although it is never alone in the person justified【798539779820781†L618-L623】[1]. The Augsburg Confession likewise teaches that people “cannot be justified before God by their own strength, merits, or works,” but are freely justified for Christ’s sake through faith when they believe that they are received into favour【141837202220372†L24-L31】[2]. Catholic theology, on the other hand, sees justification as both forensic and transformative: God not only pardons sins but also infuses grace into the soul, making the person actually righteous. The Council of Trent taught that nobody can be justified by his own works without the grace of God【700090415326814†L68-L70】[3] and condemned the notion that justification is by faith alone【700090415326814†L93-L103】[4].

Why It Matters

Justification is not an abstract debate but concerns our standing before a holy God. For Protestants, assurance of salvation rests on Christ’s completed work and the believer’s trust in Him. For Catholics, justification begins in baptism, can be increased by cooperation with grace, and can be lost through mortal sin and restored through the sacrament of penance. The way we understand justification therefore shapes our view of the sacraments, the Christian life and our relationship with God.

Doctrinal Summary

Protestant Position

Classical Protestantism holds that justification is a once‑for‑all judicial act by which God pronounces a sinner righteous because of Christ’s obedience and atoning death. Faith is the sole instrument that unites the believer to Christ; good works are the necessary fruit of faith but have no role in obtaining justification. The Westminster Confession emphasises that justifying faith “is the alone instrument of justification” and that this act is “not by anything wrought in them, or done by them”【798539779820781†L618-L623】. The Lutheran Augsburg Confession similarly states that people are “freely justified for Christ’s sake through faith”【141837202220372†L24-L31】.

Catholic Position

Catholicism teaches that justification involves both the remission of sins and the sanctification and renewal of the interior man. It begins with baptism, which the Catechism calls the gateway to the Christian life and the sacrament of regeneration【571706269541635†L21-L26】; baptism frees us from sin and makes us sons of God. The Council of Trent declared that nobody can be justified by his own works without grace【700090415326814†L68-L70】[3] and anathematised those who say that justification is by faith alone【700090415326814†L93-L103】[4]. The Catechism explains that sacramental grace not only forgives sins but “conforms us to the divine nature” and that the sacraments of the New Covenant are “necessary for salvation”【655887792400651†L40-L45】[5]. While initial justification is entirely God’s gift, Catholics hold that cooperation with grace through faith working in love is necessary for growth in righteousness. Because justification can be lost through grave sin, Christ instituted the sacrament of penance so that those who fall may be restored.

The Protestant Argument

Protestants present justification by faith alone as the heart of the gospel. They argue that:

  • All people are sinners and cannot meet God’s standard of righteousness.
  • Jesus, by His obedience and death, fulfilled the law and bore the penalty due to sinners.
  • His righteousness is imputed to believers by faith alone, apart from any works. As the Westminster Confession says, faith is the alone instrument of justification【798539779820781†L618-L623】.
  • Good works flow from faith but do not contribute to our right standing before God.

On this view, adding any requirement beyond faith—whether baptism, sacraments or works—undermines the sufficiency of Christ’s atonement and the believer’s assurance. Justification is a single event and cannot be increased or lost.

The Catholic Argument

Catholics affirm that salvation is entirely by God’s grace but maintain that grace heals and elevates our nature rather than leaving us legally covered yet internally unchanged. Justification begins with baptism, which cleanses us from sin and infuses divine life【571706269541635†L21-L26】[6]. The Council of Trent anathematised the teaching that justifying faith is merely a confidence in the divine mercy and that nothing else is required【700090415326814†L93-L103】.

Catholics argue that St Paul speaks of “faith working through love” (Gal 5:6) and that St James teaches that “a man is justified by works and not by faith alone” (Jas 2:24). The Church therefore teaches that justification is increased as we cooperate with grace. Sacraments are not human additions but means through which Christ applies His saving work. The Catechism states that the sacraments of the New Covenant are necessary for salvation because they confer the grace they signify【655887792400651†L40-L45】[5]. Baptism is ordinarily necessary for salvation【721957094790884†L536-L545】[7], yet God is not bound by His sacraments; He can act outside them, but we are bound to seek salvation through the means He established.

Finally, Catholics point out that for centuries before the Reformation, Christians understood justification as transformative. Fathers like Augustine taught that God makes us righteous by imparting His love to our hearts. The coherence of this view can be seen in the unbroken practice of infant baptism and the sacrament of penance. Far from denying Christ’s sufficiency, the Catholic position insists that it is Christ Himself who justifies believers through the sacraments and our cooperation with His grace.
